## Origin

SeeVar is named and structured in homage to the early pioneers of variable
star astronomy — particularly the group of researchers at the Harvard College
Observatory in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Under the direction of Edward Charles Pickering, a remarkable group of women
astronomers — later known informally as **Pickering's Computers** — performed
the painstaking measurements that laid the foundation of modern stellar
photometry.

Among them:

| Name | Contribution |
|------|-------------|
| **Williamina Fleming** | Discovered hundreds of variable stars while cataloguing stellar spectra |
| **Annie Jump Cannon** | Created the modern stellar classification system (OBAFGKM) |
| **Henrietta Swan Leavitt** | Discovered the period–luminosity relation of Cepheid variables, making measurement of cosmic distances possible |

Their work transformed astronomy from descriptive observation into quantitative
stellar science.

---

## Why This Matters for SeeVar

SeeVar follows the same philosophical principle:

> *Astronomy advances through consistent measurement over long periods of time.*

Variable stars are not understood through a single observation. They require
systematic monitoring, often spanning months or years. The Pickering group
achieved this with photographic plates and careful manual measurement.

SeeVar achieves the same goal using automated telescopes, deterministic control
software, and a continuous photometric pipeline.

Different tools — identical scientific philosophy.

## The Modern Equivalent of Plate Stacks

Where the Harvard astronomers worked with physical photographic plates,
SeeVar produces a modern equivalent:

- RAW FITS frames with sovereign timestamps
- AAVSO-compliant photometric measurements
- A ledger that never forgets an observation

The pipeline acts as a robotic plate-measuring assistant, ensuring that each
observation is consistent, logged, and scientifically reproducible.

---

## Scientific Philosophy

Small telescopes can produce valuable scientific data if they operate
consistently and automatically.

Automation ensures that observations occur every clear night, measurement
cadence is maintained, and human fatigue does not limit data collection.
SeeVar allows a small instrument to behave like a persistent robotic observer.
